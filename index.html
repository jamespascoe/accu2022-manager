Why this Talk?

  • Becoming a software manager is daunting
    •
  • ... but there are many benefits to doing so
    •
  • Share my experience
    •
  • Address concerns
    •
  • Debunk myths
    •
So why this talk? Well what I want to do is to point out some of the benefits of becoming a software manager. And having been through this a few times, I know it can be daunting, but what I have found is that if approached with the right mindset, moving into a Tech Leadership position can be immensely rewarding both from a personal development perspective and also the positive effects you can have on the people that you lead.

Concern 1: Will I Have to Stop Coding?

  • In a word - no. Certainly not my experience
    •
  • Solve harder problems
    •
  • Stay in touch with the code
    •
  • Team members respect coding skills
    •
  • Mentoring others means mastering your skills
    •

Concern 2: What do I do day-to-day?

  • Organise the work (short-term)
    •
    • We use Jira - other strategies work well
  • Translate commercial strategy into a roadmap (longer-term)
    •
    • Work packages and deliveries
  • Develop an appropriate amount of process
    •
    • Definition of 'done'
The first bullet is about the short-term day-to-day activities. What are we doing for the next sprint? The second is about the longer-term, so anticipating what's coming, translating that into work packages, and scheduling those within the capacity of the team.

Concern 3: How Do I Motivate Others?

  • Understand individual motivations
    •
  • Use interesting tools and libraries
    •
  • Make it fun
    •
  • Praise is free
    •
  • Track happiness
    •
Motivating people - how to get engineers to do what you ask. Engineers are, by definition, very smart people. They certainly have brains, they have ideas of their own and they have options. So, [laughs] one of the things you quickly realise as a manager is that people aren't necessarily going to do what you say, just because you say it. So, in terms of motivating people, there are lots of things you should do. For me, what I have found to be fundamental in this regard is respect.

Concern 4: Hard Conversations

  • Do happen occasionally
    •
  • Come straight to the point
    •
  • Focus on outcome
    •
  • Make it clear what needs to change
    •
  • Be fair and respectful
    •
Do happen occasionally - and it would probably be remiss of me not to mention this. Occasionally, you do have to tell somebody that despite them working 60 hour weeks all year, you can't promote them because of budget constraints. But, when you've been through a few of these, you realise that these occasions are not actually that bad and can be constructive if conducted in the right way. [bullets 2-5] You have a responsibility and to some extent a duty-of-care towards your team.

Concern 5: What Are the Benefits?

  • You can influence the technology
    •
  • Solve software engineering problems
    •
    • Versioning, Delivery, DevOps etc.
  • Commercial exposure
    •
  • Improvement in communication skills
    •
  • You can help people - training budgets, praise etc.
    •
  • You will learn about yourself and others
    •
[bullets 1-7] And just to pick up on that last bullet, one thing I discovered about myself is that I like responsibility. It sounds a bit odd, but I like the notion of saying 'actually I am going to deliver on this customer deployment' or 'business goal' or whatever. Not leaving for somebody else to think about. And the other thing that becoming a manager has done for me is this point about respect. I don't know if I am just getting older [laughs], but you realise that regardless of technical skills, seniority, profile whatever, people are people and are trying to do the right thing, they are trying to work hard balance family commitments, health issues. So, that's been particularly rewarding for me.

Conclusion

  • Technical leadership can be daunting
    •
  • But gives you different skills and mindsets
    •
  • Definitely rewarding
    •
    • When approached in the right way
  • You can always go back
    •
[bullets 1-5] And one final thing, if people higher up in the organisation think that you would be a good manager, then chances are you will be.
